Tips for a lean (not sloppy) bulk

  • 1Lift with progressive overload 3–5x/week. Without the training stimulus, the surplus just becomes fat, not muscle.
  • 2Weigh yourself daily, use the weekly average. If it's rising faster than ~0.5 kg/week, you're gaining more fat than muscle — trim calories.
  • 3Prioritize protein at every meal (25–45g) over cramming carbs — it drives muscle repair and keeps you fuller in a surplus.
  • 4Sleep 7–9 hours. Growth hormone release and muscle recovery both depend on it more than any supplement will.
  • 5Keep some daily movement (walking, steps) even while bulking — it keeps the surplus from turning into pure fat storage.
  • 6Track strength numbers, not just the scale. Weight going up with lifts stalling means the surplus isn't being used to build muscle.

Avoid these while bulking

  • ✕Don't "dirty bulk" — eating anything and everything just adds fat, not muscle. The surplus still needs to be moderate.
  • ✕Don't skip leg day or any muscle group — uneven training builds an uneven physique, not a stronger base.
  • ✕Don't judge progress by the scale alone — a big jump in a week is usually water and food weight, not muscle.
  • ✕Don't cut cardio out completely — some light activity keeps your heart healthy and the fat gain in check.
  • ✕Don't extend the bulk indefinitely — 8–12 weeks, then reassess. Endless surplus just becomes fat storage.
  • ✕Don't neglect sleep for extra late workouts — poor recovery cancels out the extra training volume.

Supplements for the bulk

  • Whey protein1–2 scoops (25–50g) daily

    Use it to close the gap to your ~150g protein target — post-workout or any meal you're short on. Not required if whole food already covers it.

  • Creatine monohydrate5g daily

    Most researched supplement for strength and muscle size. Take any time of day — consistency matters more than timing. No loading phase needed.

  • Mass gainer optional1 scoop with milk

    Only if you genuinely struggle to eat enough for a surplus. It's just extra calories and carbs in liquid form — whole food is just as good if you can manage it.

  • Multivitamin1 tablet daily

    A cheap insurance policy for micronutrients, especially if extra bulk calories are coming from less varied food.

  • Fish oil (omega-3)1–2g EPA/DHA daily

    Supports joints and heart health as training volume and load go up.

  • Caffeine optional150–200mg pre-workout

    Boosts training energy and focus. Skip if you're sensitive to it or it affects your sleep.

Run this phase 8–12 weeks, tracking bodyweight weekly. If gain exceeds ~0.5 kg/week, trim ~150–200 kcal from carbs.

Tips to actually burn fat, not muscle

  • 1Keep the deficit moderate (~400–600 kcal below maintenance). Too aggressive and you'll burn muscle along with fat.
  • 2Keep protein at ~150g+ and keep lifting — this is what signals your body to protect muscle while it burns fat for fuel.
  • 3Add daily steps (8,000–10,000) or light cardio. This burns extra fat without cutting calories further or wrecking recovery.
  • 4Don't fear fat loss "stalls" — water retention and hormonal shifts hide fat loss for days. Judge by 2-week weight trend, not daily numbers.
  • 5Drink water before meals and eat fibrous veg first — both help you feel full on fewer calories.
  • 6Save some calories for a weekly treat meal. It makes the deficit sustainable and stops the all-or-nothing crash-diet cycle.

Avoid these while cutting

  • ✕Don't slash calories too hard — an extreme deficit burns muscle along with fat and tanks your energy for training.
  • ✕Don't drop protein to save calories — cut carbs or fat first, protein is what protects your muscle in a deficit.
  • ✕Don't stop lifting to "save energy" — skipping training removes the exact signal that tells your body to keep the muscle.
  • ✕Don't chase the scale daily — water, sodium, and hormones swing it day to day. Watch the weekly average instead.
  • ✕Don't rely on cardio alone to create the deficit — food intake matters more than the minutes on a treadmill.
  • ✕Don't cut for months without a break — a maintenance break every 6–8 weeks protects your metabolism and mental energy.

Supplements for the cut

  • Whey protein1–2 scoops (25–50g) daily

    Keeps protein at ~150g even with fewer total calories to work with — often easier than fitting in that much from whole food alone in a deficit.

  • Creatine monohydrate5g daily

    Keep taking it through the cut — it helps you hold onto strength and muscle while calories are down.

  • Caffeine150–200mg pre-workout

    Helps you train hard on lower energy and can mildly curb appetite. Skip late in the day so it doesn't wreck your sleep.

  • Multivitamin1 tablet daily

    More important now than in the bulk — fewer total calories means less room for micronutrients to come from food alone.

  • Fiber (psyllium husk) optional5g with water

    Helps you feel full on fewer calories and keeps digestion steady when food volume drops.

  • Electrolytes optionalas needed

    Useful if you add more cardio or sweat more — prevents cramping and the flat, tired feeling that comes with a deficit.

Start this phase only after the bulk. Target ~0.5 kg/week loss — if it stalls two weeks running, drop another ~100–150 kcal from carbs or fat, never protein.

Muscle vs. Fat — the two levers

Building muscle and burning fat use opposite calorie settings, which is exactly why the two phases are split. These apply across both.

Gain muscle

  • 1Progressive overload is non-negotiable — add weight, reps, or sets over time or the muscle has no reason to grow.
  • 2Train each muscle group 2x/week minimum — more frequent stimulus beats one brutal weekly session.
  • 3Hit 1.6–2.2g protein per kg bodyweight daily, spread across 4–5 meals rather than one big serving.
  • 4Rest 48 hours before hitting the same muscle group hard again — growth happens during recovery, not the workout.

Burn fat

  • 1A calorie deficit is the only thing that burns fat — no food, timing, or supplement replaces that.
  • 2Keep lifting through the deficit. It's the single biggest factor in losing fat instead of muscle.
  • 3Add 2–3 short cardio or brisk-walk sessions a week to increase the deficit without cutting food further.
  • 4Manage stress and sleep — poor sleep raises cortisol and hunger hormones, making fat loss noticeably harder.

Glossary

The vocabulary behind this plan — knowing what these mean helps you adjust things yourself instead of guessing.

CutCalorie deficit

Eating fewer calories than your body burns in a day. This is the only thing that forces your body to release stored fat for energy.

BulkCalorie surplus

Eating more calories than you burn. Needed to build new muscle tissue — your body can't build much from nothing.

BothTDEE

Total Daily Energy Expenditure — the total calories your body burns in a day (breathing, digestion, movement, exercise). Your surplus or deficit is measured against this number.

BothBMR

Basal Metabolic Rate — calories burned just staying alive at rest, before any movement is added. TDEE = BMR + activity.

BulkProgressive overload

Gradually increasing the weight, reps, or sets you lift over time. Muscles only grow when they're forced to handle more than they're used to.

BulkMuscle protein synthesis

The process where your body repairs and rebuilds muscle fibers after training — using the protein you eat as raw material. This is literally "gaining muscle" at the cellular level.

BothMacronutrients (macros)

The three sources of calories: protein (muscle repair), carbohydrate (energy/fuel), and fat (hormones, cell function). Tracked in grams, not just total calories.

BothBody recomposition

Losing fat and gaining muscle at the same time. Possible for beginners, but slower and less reliable than doing a dedicated bulk then cut — which is why this plan splits the two.

CutNEAT

Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis — calories burned from daily movement that isn't a workout (walking, fidgeting, chores). A major, underrated fat-loss lever.

CutRefeed day

A planned day of eating at maintenance calories during a cut, used to ease diet fatigue and support training performance without derailing fat loss.

BothDOMS

Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness — the ache 24–48 hours after a hard workout. Normal and expected; not a required sign that a workout "worked."

BothMaintenance calories

The calorie amount at which your weight stays roughly stable — the baseline both the bulk (above it) and cut (below it) are measured from.

This is a general structure, not individual medical or dietetic advice. Swap proteins/carbs for foods you actually like and can sustain — adherence beats the "perfect" plan every time. Supplements support the plan, they don't replace it — food and training come first. If you have any health conditions, take medication, or are pregnant/breastfeeding, check with a doctor before starting any supplement.
